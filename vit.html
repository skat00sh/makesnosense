Learning Vision Transformers(Without the Language-Transformer Proxy)

Every figure is a toy you can poke · flip a card to meet the paper

Almost every explanation of the Vision Transformer I’ve come across reaches for the language transformer first, patches become “tokens, like words,” attention is “the same idea as in BERT.” That’s a natural route, and probably how most of us first met the model: it’s how the research actually moved, transformers winning at language well before vision. But it always felt to me a little like a proxy, explaining a model whose input is an image by way of one whose input is text. This is my attempt at the other path: building a Vision Transformer from the image upward: patches, numbers, tokens, position, attention, entirely by hand (with Claude’s help), on one small picture of a tree, with as little borrowed from the language as was needed.

How to read it: every stage below is a live widget. Poke it. Where the paper has something precise to say, the card flips over to show how the toy lines up with the real thing.

The whole machine, on one screen

How a Vision Transformer sees an image

Our job is easy to say and surprisingly involved to do: look at this little block-built tree from Lego Land and decide it's a tree. Six stages get us there. Click any block, or press play, to see what each does; the rest of the post builds them by hand, small enough to check on paper.

So that’s the whole map. Now we walk it, starting exactly where the model starts, by refusing to look at the picture as a picture.

Section · Patches

The network never sees a tree. It sees numbers.

Here's the thing the colored picture hides: a computer has no notion of "green." Every pixel is just three numbers: how much red, green, and blue light it carries, each from 0 to 255. Stack those three layers, let your eye mix the light, and you get the color you see. Cutting the image into patches is nothing more than grouping these numbers into tiles. Our toy is a 6×6 image, 36 pixels, sliced into a 3×3 grid of nine 2×2 patches (4 pixels each). Flip the view and pick a patch to see the only thing the model ever gets.

So a patch isn't a little colored square. It's a small stack of numbers. Next we squash each patch's numbers down into a single vector, its "token," so the rest of the machine has something compact to work with.

From the paper · patches

Cutting the image into tokens

To hand an image to a plain Transformer, ViT first slices it into a grid of fixed-size, non-overlapping patches and treats each one as a token. That single move, no convolutions, just a reshape, is what lets a text-style architecture read pixels at all. Patch size is a knob: 16×16 in ViT-Base, 2×2 in our toy (and in Cordonnier 2020, the closest precursor).

Twelve numbers per patch is more than the rest of the machine wants to carry. So each patch gets squeezed down to a single short vector, its token, by a couple of hand-picked detectors.

Section · Tokens · the toy

Squashing a patch into a single vector

This is the step that earns its keep. A patch is 12 loose numbers; the rest of the machine wants one tidy vector per patch, a token. Two moves get us there. Flatten: unroll the patch into a line (just rearranging). Embed: take weighted sums of those numbers to get a few meaningful features. The embedding below is two detectors we picked by hand so you can do the math yourself. Pick a patch, then work out its averages.

Move 3 · Token space

Every patch is now one point in a 2-number space. But these points carry no clue about where each patch sat in the picture. That gap is the next move, adding position.

From the paper · §3.1 & Eq. 1

What the real ViT does here

The paper keeps it deliberately plain. An H×W×C image is cut into N square patches of side P, and each patch is flattened to a length-(P²·C) vector. A single trainable matrix E maps every flattened patch to the model's fixed width D, these are the “patch embeddings,” the paper's name for our tokens. Two things our toy skipped: a learnable [class] token is prepended to serve as the final image summary, and learnable 1-D position vectors are added on top. The key difference: E isn't hand-designed for greenness and redness, it's learned end-to-end. Its weights are still plain numbers you can inspect; they're just not labeled for you, so making sense of them takes interpretability tools (the paper visualizes E and finds its leading components look like low-level filters).

Each patch is a token now, but the tokens arrive in a heap with no memory of where their patch sat. To everything downstream, that heap is just a set: shuffle it and nothing changes. So position has to be stitched back in by hand.

Section · Position · the toy

Where was each patch, and why can't the model tell?

In the last step we saw, every patch became a token Z, and that vector is all the model ever sees of that patch. Stack them up and the image is just a bag of Z's with no order. Attention reads that bag the same however it's arranged, so any two images with the same set of Z's are identical to the model, including every shuffle of our tree. With position off, shuffle and watch the bag on the right never change. Now, turn on add sinusoidal, pick a slot, and shuffle again. Watch its coordinates move this time. The network only ever sees coordinates, so this is the only difference it can possibly notice.

The position code · how sin & cos make a circle

Two choices, both just for this toy: 40° is 360°÷9, one full turn split evenly across the 9 slots, so every patch gets its own spot on the circle. 28 is the circle's radius, chosen so the position code is a clearly visible nudge, about a sixth of our tokens' overall spread (about −54 to 116, a range of 170), enough to noticeably shift a token without overwhelming the content it already carries.

Now every token carries both what and where. That's the complete input, and the attention stage is finally ready to chew on it. That's next.

From the paper · position

Telling the model where each patch was

A pile of patch tokens has no inherent order, and self-attention is permutation-invariant: swap two tokens and the output just swaps with them, nothing else changes. So position has to be added in. ViT does it with a learnable vector per slot, the + Epos in Equation 1. In the paper's own words they use “standard learnable 1D position embeddings” [1], and report no real gain from fancier 2D-aware ones.

How does a “learnable” Epos actually learn?

It begins as random vectors, one per slot, that say nothing about where each patch sits. During training they ride along with every other weight under gradient descent: whenever a slot's vector helps the model land the right label, that nudge sticks; when it hurts, it's pushed back. The paper notes these vectors start out carrying no information about 2D layout, so every spatial relationship has to be learned from scratch. By the end, neighbouring slots have drifted toward similar vectors, the grid is rediscovered, never hard-coded.

The sinusoidal alternative: what our toy mimics (Vaswani 2017)

PE(pos, 2i)  = sin( pos / 100002i/d )
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos( pos / 100002i/d )
Fixed, not learned. ViT follows Vaswani's Transformer encoder closely [1], but for position it picks learnable codes over these. The toy uses sin/cos because you can work them out by hand.

Section · Attention · the setup

Every patch is an island. Attention builds the bridges.

After the last steps each patch is a token that knows its own colour and position, and nothing else. A lone green square can't tell leaf from grass from a green door. To recognise a tree, patches have to share what they see. Attention is how each patch polls every other patch and rewrites itself with what it learns. Two questions are still open: how is a Query actually built, and what counts as a good match with a Key? The next two cards answer that with real numbers: first the obvious rule (match), then the surprising one (bind).

From the paper · the three projections

Query, Key, Value: three views of one token

In a real Transformer, each token is pushed through three separate learned matrices to play three roles at once. The query asks, the key answers, the value is what gets passed on. Attention scores every query against every key, softmaxes the scores into weights, and returns the weighted sum of values.

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q KT / √dk ) V
Q = X WQ ,   K = X WK ,   V = X WV
X = the patch tokens; WQ, WK, WV are learned. √dk keeps the scores from saturating the softmax.

What each role means

Query · Q
What am I looking for? The question this patch broadcasts.
Key · K
What do I offer? The label each patch holds up to be matched against.
Value · V
What I hand over. The content a patch contributes when attended to.

Because Q and K come from different matrices, a patch's question need not look like its own label, which is exactly what lets it attend to things unlike itself. Our toy keeps K and V as the raw token and only changes the query, so across the next cards you can watch that one idea do all the work.

Start with the obvious rule. A patch could simply ask: who else looks like me?

Section · Attention · rule 1 of 2

Match: pay attention to whoever’s most like you

The simplest possible query: a patch just uses itself as the question. Then “relevance” means how alike are we?, and the measure of alikeness is the dot product of the two tokens. Big and positive when they point the same way, negative when they're opposites. Pick an asker, then compute one score yourself.

Section · Attention · rule 2 of 2

Bind: rotate the question, and a patch can seek its opposite

Match only ever pulls together things that are alike. But a tree isn't made of identical patches. It's green canopy and brown trunk, two tokens pointing opposite ways. For the canopy to gather the trunk, its query must point somewhere other than itself. The trick is to rotate the query: now “what I look for” is no longer “what I am.” Drag the dial and watch where the canopy's attention goes.

Where does “rotate the query” come from?
In a real Transformer the query isn't the token, it's Q = X·WQ, a learned linear map. Linear maps can rotate, scale and shear, so the network is free to learn a query that points wherever is useful, including straight at a patch's opposite. Our fixed 90° turn is just the simplest such map, hard-coded so you can see the mechanism, real models discover their own from data, which is how a canopy detector learns to go looking for trunks.

One knob, the query's rotation, turns “find my twins” into “find what completes me.” That's the whole reason Q and K are separate, learned things. Next we put match and bind together and watch a few patches rewrite each other into something that finally knows it's a tree.

Neither question alone is the answer: real attention asks several at once, in parallel, and combines what comes back. Here's both of ours, running together.

From the paper · attention

Self-attention & multi-head, mapped to the toy

Each token is projected into a query, key and value. Every query is dotted with every key, scaled and softmaxed into weights, and the token is rewritten as the weighted sum of values. Several heads do this in parallel, different learned questions, and their outputs are concatenated and projected back. It's global from the first layer: in ViT the “self-attention layers are global” [2].

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q KT / √dk ) V
Q = X WQ ,   K = X WK ,   V = X WV
MultiHead = Concat(head1, …, headh) WO
√dk stops the dot products saturating the softmax. ViT-Base: h = 12 heads, width D = 768.

One more move, and we're done. Everything attention built up, colour, position, and now a little of every other patch, gets squashed into a single vector and handed to the simplest possible reader: a straight line.

Section · Classify · the toy

Squash everything into one vector, and read off the label

Nine tokens, each carrying a little colour and a little position, now also carry a little of each other, that's what the attention layer did. The last move is the simplest in the whole pipeline: average all nine tokens into one, and feed that single vector to a small classifier. That's the whole pipeline. Pixels → patches → tokens → positions → attention → one vector → a number → a label. Nothing in here ever saw a "tree". Only coordinates, dot products, and a weighted sum that happens to land on the right side of zero once attention has done its job.

From the paper · classification head

The [class] token and the MLP head

ViT doesn't average every token. It prepends one extra learnable token, [class], to the sequence before the first layer. After all the attention layers, that one token's final vector is treated as a summary of the whole image, and a small MLP head turns it into class scores.

y = MLP( LN( zL0 ) )
zL0 = the [class] token's vector after the last (L-th) layer; LN = layer norm; MLP = a small feed-forward head → class logits.

Hopefully this gave you an intuitive feel for the attention mechanism inside a Vision Transformer, built from one small image, no language detour required. From here, the original papers should read a lot less like a foreign language.

A few real ideas got left out along the way, deliberately. Not because they don't matter, building intuition for one mechanism was the goal, and dragging in every piece of the architecture would have made this very long. Worth knowing about when you go further:

  • Residual connections. Attention's output doesn't replace a token, it's added to it. Each layer nudges the tokens rather than overwriting them, which is part of why deep stacks are trainable at all.
  • Layer normalization. Before each attention and MLP step, every token is rescaled to a consistent range. It keeps the numbers well-behaved as they pass through many layers.
  • Multi-head attention. Real ViT runs many heads in parallel, twelve in ViT-Base, each asking a different question, then combines the results. Our toy's two heads (match and bind) are a stand-in for that whole bank.
  • The [class] token. ViT prepends one extra learnable token to the sequence. It starts with no special meaning and only becomes a useful summary because it's trained, end to end, to be one. ViT also stacks 12 to 32 of these layers depending on model size, each refining the same set of tokens a little further.
